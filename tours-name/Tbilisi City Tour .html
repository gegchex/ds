Tbilisi City Tour Walking + entertainment

General Tour Description:

Tbilisi is a city of dynamic contrasts and endless possibilities. Discover its rich history at ancient sites and active religious centers, then dive into the vibrant local culture with mouthwatering Georgian cuisine and exceptional beverages. For a touch of excitement, experience the thrilling rides at Mtatsminda amusement park, or explore the scenic trails in the surrounding mountains. If you’re seeking relaxation, unwind with a yoga class or relax in renowned bath houses, where traditional sulfur baths provide a soothing escape. As night falls, Tbilisi’s vibrant nightlife comes alive with bustling bars and casinos, offering an electric mix of entertainment and glamour. Whether you’re drawn to history, architecture, nature, food, adventure, entertainment or night life, Tbilisi has something for everyone.

  • Walking tour:
  • Metekhi church;

    Constructed in XIII c., The Metekhi Church (also known as The Metekhi church of the Nativity of the Mother of God) is not just a place of religious worship but a historical landmark that stands as a testament to the enduring Georgian spirit. The site offers stunning panoramic views of the old Tbilisi and the river Kura (Mtkvari). The contrast between the church's serene, historic setting and the bustling streets—filled with colorful markets, lively cafes, and a mix of modern and traditional architecture—captures the dynamic spirit of Tbilisi in all its glory.

  • Meidan Bazar;

    Hidden beneath Meidan Square in old Tbilisi, Meidan Bazaar is a charming underground market offering a wide range of traditional Georgian goods. Visitors can explore historic trade tunnel to find local wines, spices, handicrafts, and souvenirs. Meidan Bazaar reflects role of Tbilisi as a key stop on ancient trade routes, making it a must-visit spot for both shopping and experiencing Georgian culture.

  • Bath house district;

    The Bathhouse District in Old Tbilisi, known as Abanotubani, is renowned for its historic sulfur springs and distinctive brick domes. This area deeply blends history with relaxation. Beneath its iconic brick domes, natural sulfur springs bubble up in Tbilisi Bath houses, offering soothing, therapeutic waters that have drawn visitors for centuries. Services include soothing massages and pilling (exfoliation), where attendants scrub away dead skin, leaving it refreshed. Many bathhouses also feature steam rooms and hot stone areas for added relaxation. The experience is often complemented with beverages, making it a truly rejuvenating and culturally rich experience.

  • Legvtakhevi and waterfall;

    Leghvtakhevi is a historic district in Tbilisi, nestled in a narrow gorge through which a small river - Tsavkistskali flows. The area is renowned for its picturesque 22-meter-high waterfall, offering a refreshing and peaceful space right in the middle of the bustling old city.

  • Tbilisi Juma Mosque;

    Built in the 19th century,
    Tbilisi’s Juma Mosque is an architectural marvel that blends Neo-Gothic and Islamic design elements. Uniquely, it serves as a shared space for both Sunni and Shiite Muslims, making it one of the few mosques in the world where members of both branches of Islam pray together in harmony. Juma Mosque symbolizes Georgia’s rich tradition of religious tolerance and unity.

  • Tbilisi synagogue;

    Situated in the heart of Tbilisi’s historic quarter,the Tbilisi Synagogue, built in the 19th century, showcases a striking blend of Eclectic, Moorish, and Romanesque Revival architectural styles. Adjacent to the active synagogue are a mikveh and a Jewish educational center. Serving as the spiritual center for Georgia’s Jewish community—one of the oldest in the country, with roots tracing back to the 6th century BC—the synagogue reflects the deep and enduring presence of Jewish heritage in Georgia

  • the Armenian Cathedral;

    Built in the 13th century, the Armenian Cathedral of Saint George stands as a spiritual and cultural center for the Armenian community in Georgia. One of the largest and oldest diasporas in the country, the Armenian presence in Georgia dates back to the 2nd century BC. The cathedral continues to serve as a testament to the centuries-old history and deep roots of the Armenian community within Georgia.

  • Catholic Cathedral (on request only);

    Built in the 19th century, the Catholic Church showcases a harmonious blend of Baroque and Neo-Gothic architectural elements. In the 20th century, it underwent restoration and was rededicated to the Assumption of Virgin Mary. Today, the sanctuary features striking contemporary frescoes. The church continues to serve as the spiritual heart for the Catholic community, offering a place of worship and spiritual reflection for believers.

  • Peace Bridge;

    Designed by Italian architect Michele De Lucchi in 2010, the Peace Bridge in Tbilisi connects the modern and historic districts of Tbilisi. In total, 48,000 LEDs and 240 sensors light up the bridge, making it a stunning visual landmark. Every hour, a Morse code message is transmitted across the parapets, symbolizing "life and peace between people," as envisioned by lighting designer Philippe Martinaud. The Peace Bridge is a symbol of unity across cultures and generations.

  • Cable ride;

    The cable car connects Rike park to Narikala. It was designed and built by the Italian company LEITNER. The entire cable car system meets international standards and European safety norms. Length of the cable line is 500 meters and features seven gondolas with panoramic view. Strategic location of the cable route makes it one of the favorite attractions among the locals as well as the tourists.

  • Narikala Fortress;

    Early evidence of Narikala Fortress dates back from the 4th century. Narikala Fortress has witnessed the evolution of Tbilisi through its early years. It has endured the city's many challenges and wars, standing as a resilient symbol of Tbilisi’s turbulent history. Although now Narikala Fortress remains one of the city's main attractions, proudly overlooking the old district of the capital.

  • Mother of Georgia monument;

    The Kartlis Deda (Mother of Georgia) monument, created by renowned Georgian sculptor Elguja Amashukeli, stands as a striking 20-meter-tall aluminum figure. Dressed in traditional Georgian attire, the statue holds a glass of wine in one hand, symbolizing the country's hospitality to friendly visitors, and a sword - in the other, representing its readiness to defend against adversaries. This monument embodies the aspects of Georgian character: warmth and hospitality, combined with strength and vigilance in protecting their land.

  • Tbilisi Botanical garden (on request);

    Nestled at the foothills of Sololaki Mountain, the Tbilisi Botanical Garden stretches over 97 hectares. Originally established in the 17th century as a royal garden, currently it features around 800 species of trees and approximately, 3,500 plant varieties. The Roller Coaster Zipline has been thrilling riders since June 2017 in Botanical Garden. The zipline stretches 270 meters and offers a 30-second adrenaline-packed ride from Sololaki hill to the Botanical Garden. Zip line operates daily, including weekends.

  • Drive on Rustaveli avenue;

    Central Avenue in Tbilisi, named after the renowned 12th-century writer - Shota Rustaveli, stretches over 1.5 kilometers and serves as a vibrant hub of governmental, public, cultural, and business activities. On this avenue, you'll find significant landmarks such as the Parliament of Georgia, the Georgian National Opera and Ballet Theatre, the Rustaveli State Academic Theatre, and the Georgian Academy of Sciences, Kashveti Church, the Georgian Museum of Fine Arts, the National Gallery, and the Georgian National Museum. All the listed above makes Rustaveli Avenue a key cultural and historical corridor in the city.

  • Visit Cathedral of Holy trinity Church;

    The Holy Trinity Cathedral, one of the largest religious buildings in the world, was designed by Georgian architect Archil Mindiashvili. Its architecture masterfully integrates various Georgian architectural styles into a cohesive and striking design. Standing 87.1 meters tall and extending 13 meters underground, the cathedral is a cherished landmark for both locals and visitors, admired for its grandeur and unique style.

  • Visit Mtatsminda Amusement park (on request).

    Perched atop Mount Mtatsminda, overlooking the Georgian capital of Tbilisi, Mtatsminda Park offers a variety of attractions, including carousels, water slides, a roller coaster, and a dark ride. The park is also accessible by funicular ride. The final station of the funicular route is located within a building complex that houses four distinct restaurants, offering visitors a variety of dining options alongside stunning views of Tbilisi. Large Ferris wheel (65 m high) situated at the mountain's edge provides breathtaking panoramic views from a height of 790 meters.
